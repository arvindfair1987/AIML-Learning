## **1. Historical Simulation VaR + Judgement Adjustment**

### **1. Compute the 99% VaR (Historical)**

250 trading days ‚Üí 1% worst = **2.5th worst day**
We look for the **third worst** loss in the data.

Given: losses > $8m happened on **2 days**.

Thus, **99% VaR = ‚Äì$8m**.

---

### **2. Judgemental Adjustment**

The risk manager believes geopolitical tensions increase downside risk.

They may adjust the VaR from **$8m ‚Üí $9m or $10m** based on expert judgement.

---

### **3. Why historical data may underestimate current risk**

* Geopolitical shocks are **forward-looking**, not captured in historical data
* Market dynamics change
* Historical windows may exclude crisis periods
* Fat-tail risks and structural breaks may be missed

---

---

## **2. Monte Carlo Simulation: Volatility Regime Shift**

### **1. 15% volatility came from relative frequency**

Historical return data ‚Üí standard deviation = 15%
This is empirical probability.

---

### **2. Judgemental update to 28%**

Implied volatility reflects market fear today.
The analyst overrides the historical value based on:

* current news
* expert opinion
* market expectations

---

### **3. Impact on simulations**

Using **28% instead of 15%**:

* Larger price swings
* Higher VaR
* Higher option prices
* Fatter tails
* Increased probability of large losses

---

---

## **3. Conditional VaR / Expected Shortfall**

### **1. Compute 95% ES**

Worst 5% of 250 days = **12.5 ‚âà 12 days**

Given average of worst 12 losses = **‚Äì$9.5m**

$
ES_{95} = -9.5 \text{ million}
$

---

### **2. VaR vs ES**

* **VaR**: threshold loss
* **ES**: *average* of losses beyond VaR ‚Üí captures **tail severity**
* ES > VaR ‚Üí More conservative

---

### **3. Probability Approaches Used**

* Losses (data) ‚Üí **Relative Frequency**
* Tail estimation often requires model judgement ‚Üí **Judgemental**
